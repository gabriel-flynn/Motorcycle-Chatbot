The Honda VFR800i is the best version of the best motorcycle ever built according to some.

  • Related: Honda VFR used buying guide

The VFR800i is a consumate all-rounder that looks great and goes better whether you’re on a track day, commuting or heading on a two week holiday with pillion and luggage. Honda VFR800i is an evolution of the VFR750, which started life as a sports bike but ended as a super sports tourer.

Ride quality & brakes

Next up: Engine
4 out of 5 (4/5)

The Honda VFR800i is capable and stable in any situation. Nimble enough for the road no matter how fast your mates are. One step more towards the touring camp than say, a CBR600F. 90% of the time that’s great but if the fast group on track days is your aim, a Honda VFR800i is probably not the best machine for you.

Engine

Next up: Reliability
4 out of 5 (4/5)

V-four layout is unusual – it’s more costly and no more powerful than the all-conquering in-line four. But the Honda VFR800i feels and sounds far more exciting and produces more grunt low in the rev range. Competitors like Triumph’s Sprint beat the Honda VFR800i for power and torque, though.

From the MCN forums...

Q. I have a Honda VFR800FI, a 1998 model. The front header pipe has snapped right at the point it joins to the cylinder head so I've had to invest in the whole downpipe collector unit.

I've never had my bike apart but I have a workshop full of tools and six mechanics to help me out so I should be all right, I've looked through Honda’s workshop manual and I can't find anything about the studs anywhere in it but I’ve heard I may need to replace them, how many do I need and what sizes?

Any other tips and advice on this would be great.
Jake, MCN forums.

A. It comes to all VFR800s sooner or later. If you buy a VFR without a stainless replacement chances are you'll be fitting one. It would be a good idea to replace the studs/nuts while you have the bike apart as they are not expensive, but the studs are only 6mm thick and snap oh so easily. If it was a rear cylinder one that snapped you would have the devil of a job replacing it.

The best bet is to carefully remove the exhaust nuts and leave the studs where they are if they are still serviceable, giving them a good clean. The old VFR400 NC21 had very tall stainless 10mm hex head cap nuts fitted to the front two downpipes as standard. Unlike a standard open-headed nut they don’t corrode as much.

See if you can pick them up from a grey import dealer or David Silver spares, and Copaslip them to avoid future seizures. We would also use new clamps and gaskets.

After all, it's a big job and if you fit a stainless system it will last forever, so you'll have peace of mind knowing you (hopefully) will never have to touch it again.

 

MarcusMarsh: I have a 1998 VFR800 with 38,000 miles on the clock and the suspension (and rear shock in particular) is past its best, so this winter it's time for refurbishment. I would like to take your opinion on a replacement rear shock. The choices I am looking at are either a new OE Honda shock, which I can source for just over £200 or a Hagon item at nearer £300. Is the Hagon worth the extra? I will be re-building the forks as well and will probably use Hagon springs for that.

kcmc: It really does depend on how you ride the bike. If you ride it at a sensible speed or a bit quicker, then the suspension is adequate. But as soon as you start push on, you have to set the rear shock up wrong to get it to work. The rear spring is too light (I think an extra kg would help here) so needs to be set at its hardest, which doesn't allow for any sag. If it's set one notch down it's too soft and will wallow like a pig (even with a new original shock). So if you ride hard, I would go for the best rear shock you can afford without breaking the bank. A Hagon shock is better than stock, but for another 100 quid or so I would fit a Maxton shock (www.maxton.netspinners.co.uk/). The forks are surprisingly good, but you can improve these also by fitting straight rate springs to replace the progressive type fitted. If you buy these and the shock off Maxton (and probably other manufacturers as well) they will match the springs to your fully clothed weight, so it all works a lot better.

MarcusMarsh: Thanks for the advice. Yes, I suppose I should have mentioned riding style. I'm 13.5 stone and use the bike for all things, including an annual tour of Spain (isn't that what VFRs are for?). I hardly ever carry a passenger and would describe my riding as brisk rather than full-on. I'll check out the Maxton site.
knightmoves: You may want to investigate fitting a Blade shock from a 929 or 954. You can pick up a second-hand one quite cheap and they have more adjustment then a stock VFR shock with only a bit of fabricating to do. (Try

Reliability & build quality

Next up: Value
4 out of 5 (4/5)

Not as good as people would have you believe – but still well above average. Rectifier issue (Honda’s Achillies heel) can cause the Honda VFR800i to stop, potentially with a boiling battery. Non-Honda units are cheap and solve the problem before it occurs – later Honda units also work well on the Honda VFR800i.

Value vs rivals

Next up: Equipment
3 out of 5 (3/5)

As it’s the pick of the Honda VFR750/800s used values are high. The later Honda VFR800 V-Tec model was heavier and needlessly complex. People know Honda VFR800is last and are happy paying top money for clean bikes, even with average or above mileages. At least you shouldn’t lose much when you sell. Find a Honda VFR800i for sale.

Equipment

5 out of 5 (5/5)

Honda VFR800i carries rider and pillion better than most machines except dedicated tourers and some big trailies. Superb headlights and decent mirrors. Honda VFR800i centre stand is nice in these weight conscious days. Clocks include fuel gauge and ambient temperature – impressive for the time the Honda VFR800i was made.

5 out of 5 One of the Finest Motorcycles Ever Produced.
24 April 2020 by PAD

Version: FiY

Year: 2000

Annual servicing cost: £100

Fitting Maxton suspension front and rear made the bike more like it should have been from the factory. A couple of hundred more cc and more torque would have been nice too. I’ve had the VFR for 13 years and will never part with it. It’s such a brilliantly capable machine that, even now, I find much more recent models a bit disappointing one way or another. I know many think of it as an old man’s bike, but how come I frequently find myself showing a clean pair of heels to litre plus sportsbikes and other flavour of the month models? And others are to be heard telling of its lack of character... A fine handling V4 with direct racing heritage... Characterless? I reckon anyone who moans about any motorcycle being bland is probably saying much more about them than the machine! I certainly would recommend a VFR800 to a friend. A few mods make it a hell of a bike.

Ride quality & brakes 4 out of 5

The bike is one of THE great all-rounders. Happy everywhere, it’s as good for commuting as it is long, fast, high mileage days on challenging roads. The brakes are fine - some are dubious or dismissive of the linked brakes, but I’ve never had a problem with the linking, even on snow and ice where I’d tend to use the rear only sometimes. Which, contrary to the belief of some, you can do with this system under light braking. Undeniably, compared to modern machines, the brakes are a bit lacking, but still adequate for my needs.

Engine 5 out of 5

The engine is awesome! Yes it would be even better with more capacity and torque, but against that is the fact that you are encouraged to rev the bike and use the gears, when the midrange becomes quite intoxicating and with a soundtrack to match. Low speed and transition from off to on throttle can be a bit jerky, but not terrible. Balancing the starter valves if it starts to become more noticeable helps. As does dumping the PAIR valve system.

Reliability & build quality 4 out of 5

Generally speaking, yes, it’s very well built. And the engine is a superb bit of engineering. Finish wise, I think the 750’s are better, but the 800 is still excellent. The bike’s 20 years old and has some corrosion on the clutch cover, the exhaust system looks awful but is actually fine, and the fairing fasteners are more marked than corroded. The rear subframe has been repainted, as have the fork legs (several times). The wheels were powder coated several years ago but need refinishing again - take care who you get to do them!In more recent years I’ve treated the bike twice a year with ACF50 after deep cleaning - well worthwhile. My bike is what would be considered high mileage (a bit shy of 70,000 miles, so about run in) has been used year round for most of its life and, yes, it’s a bit dog eared for sure. But it’s a 20 year old workhorse that’s been ridden hard and not at all pampered. That it’s still up and not only running, but running better than ever is a testament to the VFR’s quality.

Value vs rivals 4 out of 5

I do, and always have done, my own servicing, maintenance and repairs so costs are small. The VFR isn’t the easiest bike to work on. Need to remove plastics for most things is a pain, the engine configuration places a lot of stuff inaccessible, between front and rear cylinder banks. And whoever sited the thermostat so the throttle bodies need to be removed should be tortured or castrated... Preferably both.

Equipment 4 out of 5

The standard bike is let down badly by its budget suspension. I found it inclined to shake its head and be very upset by white lines, banding and road defects when getting a move on. Handling is great with the Maxton mods and with everything well set up it’s certainly a match for many more modern/more sporty bikes. Other mods I,ve made include (as stated above) removing the PAIR valve system. Replacing the whole charging system (after it failed on mine) included a Shindengen FH020AA MOSFET regulator/rectifier and wiring direct to the battery (completely bypassing the original setup) with substantially beefed up wiring and electronic protection should be considered essential. As should investigating, uncovering and removing a pointless earth block buried in the loom near the r/r. Not only will that avoid (or cure) a number of odd electrical gremlins, but the bike will feel altogether smoother for it. I’ve wired the bike for all sorts of accessories (heated kit, electric supply to a tank bag, USB for camera and various other outlets, all running via a Rowe PDM60 electronic power distribution module - not cheap, but a brilliant bit of kit. I also replaced the standard horn with a pair of Stebel Magnums. Not as aggressive sounding as the Nautilus - more... authoritative! Even truckers get out of the way. And I love watching the tester’s reaction at MOT time! A hugger should be considered essential, and a fender extender too. I have a Gidibi chain oiler fitted (the Cobra Nemo is identical apart from being badged and about 3 times the price). This was easily fitted and works way better than a certain well known UK device, which seemed to like putting more oil on the rear tyre than the chain, even when turned right down! Tyre choice? Dunlop Roadsmart III are a very, very good choice. Superb performance in all conditions, predictable, agile and decent tyre life. That said, out of curiosity, I have fitted a pair of Conti Road Attack 3s and really like what I’ve found since, but time will tell, and I’ll always be very happy to revert to the Dunlops.

Buying experience: I bought from a local Honda dealer 13 years ago. Price seems irrelevant due to the time since. Knowing what I now know about the VFR, I wouldn’t have any qualms about buying privately, or buying a high mileage bike once it had clearly been treated well and serviced correctly. These days, they can be had for little money and, in decent condition, are a steal.
